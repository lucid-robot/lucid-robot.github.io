We learn a manipulation intent model from human video and a tracking policy from simulation, and pair them in the real world on a dexterous hand and a parallel-jaw gripper.

Abstract

The most widely-adopted robot learning pipelines today learn skills from robot demonstrations or structured human data, which are expensive to collect and tied to specific embodiments. In contrast, unstructured human videos provide a scalable alternative. They contain diverse manipulation demonstrations across objects, scenes, and strategies, but are not directly connected to robot action. We propose LUCID, a two-stage framework that learns task intent from unstructured human videos drawn from internet-scale datasets and learns robot control in massively-parallel simulation. The intent model predicts short-horizon intent (what should happen next in the scene) from the current observation in closed loop. An embodiment-specific tracking policy converts this intent into robot actions. The intent interface is shared across controllers, so the same intent model can be applied to different embodiments, from our primary dexterous hand to a parallel-jaw gripper. We evaluate LUCID on five real-world manipulation tasks: stirring, wiping, and binning supervised by only internet video, with zero-shot transfer to novel scenes and object instances; and push-T and cable routing supervised by 1 hr each of self-collected smartphone video.

Intent–control split

An intent model trained on human video predicts what should happen next; a tracking policy trained in simulation handles how the robot does it.

Closed-loop intent

Intent is re-queried continually from the live scene rather than planned once, so the robot recovers when execution diverges.

Embodiment-agnostic

The same intent interface drives both a dexterous hand and a parallel-jaw gripper, with no per-embodiment human data.

Method

Stage 1 — Intent model

From a single RGB-D observation, the intent model predicts short-horizon object flow and a rough palm pose. It is embodiment-agnostic and trained purely on unstructured human video — no robot action labels, no object meshes, no motion capture.

Stage 2 — Tracking policy

A goal-conditioned policy trained once in massively-parallel simulation realizes the intent reference on a specific embodiment. We train a teacher on privileged state and distill a student that runs from onboard sensing, with domain randomization and a curriculum for robust sim-to-real transfer.

Real-world rollouts

Five tasks. Stirring, wiping, and binning are supervised by internet video only; push-T and cable routing by 1 hr each of smartphone video.

Closed-loop vs. open-loop

Closed-loop LUCID re-queries intent from the live scene and recovers from disturbances; an open-loop planner commits to a single plan at t=0.

Zero-shot generalization

The intent model is supervised on internet video and transfers zero-shot to novel scenes, camera viewpoints, and object instances it never saw.

Scaling with video

Performance improves predictably with the amount of training video — both held-out intent loss and downstream real-world success.

Emergent behaviors

Closed-loop intent yields behaviors we never explicitly supervised — recovering from slips, re-approaching, and composing sub-skills within a single rollout.
